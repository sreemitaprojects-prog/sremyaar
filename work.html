Jugaad

A crowdsourced vendor map for college fest organisers across India.

visit project →

College fests in India run on a parallel economy that nobody has ever properly mapped. Every organiser figures it out from scratch: who prints the banners, who rigs the lights, who will set up a stall for less than the budget allows. The information exists, scattered across seniors' phones and WhatsApp groups and pure word of mouth, and it disappears every time a batch graduates.

I built Jugaad because I watched this happen and thought someone should just write it down somewhere permanent. It is a crowdsourced vendor map for college fest organisers across five Indian cities. You find a vendor, you add a vendor, the next batch inherits what you learned.

I built it in a weekend while juggling a political science assignment and a content deadline. That felt right somehow.

I spent a month in Mumbai for a summer internship and spent a lot of that time trying to figure out where I was welcome. Not in a dramatic sense, just in the everyday sense of being a young woman alone in a city that is not hers, looking for a cafe to work from, a bookshop to wander into, a space that felt like it was made with her in mind.

The information existed but it was not organised anywhere. It lived in Instagram saves and recommendations from friends of friends and the occasional article that was already two years old.

PinD is a community map of women-owned spaces in India, starting with Mumbai. Built for the woman who wants to know where she is welcome before she arrives. I am a full time college student who built this on the side, which means it is not perfect, but it is real and it is growing and anyone can add to it.

A media and speaker platform for urban Indian working women.

I found Decoding Draupadi before I knew what I was looking for. I was seventeen and trying to understand what it meant to be a young woman in India with opinions and ambitions and nowhere obvious to put them. The platform was building exactly the kind of space I had been looking for without knowing I was looking for it.

I joined as a full time college student studying political science, which turned out to be less of a contradiction than it sounds. The work I do here lives at the same intersection my degree does: power, voice, who gets to speak and who gets to be heard. I write, build products, work on strategy, run events, and contribute to editorial. I helped produce our first book, a collection of literary journalism essays on women's speeches that changed the world. I helped build our flagship summit, Draupadi on Record.

This is not an internship I am passing through. This is the work I am trying to do.
